What people notice first A message that arrives looking routine -- the right name, the right format -- until it asks for something specific.
What scammers want A click, a code, a login, or a payment made before the sender or the destination has been independently checked.
Why it feels believable The sender name or logo matches something real. The address or domain behind it does not.
What makes it hard to catch The tell is always in the from address, the link destination, or the form field that should not be there.

The message appeared with the display name "real company," a trusted brand familiar to the recipient. At first glance, it seemed legitimate, bearing the company’s logo and branding colors. However, the from address was a random domain, completely unrelated to the actual company’s official email servers. This disconnect between the display name and the sender’s address was subtle but unmistakable on closer inspection.

The body of the email claimed there had been a recent login attempt that the user never initiated. It referenced a specific action—an attempted payment of $1,250—that the recipient supposedly made. The subject line read, "Urgent: Unauthorized Payment Alert," designed to prompt immediate attention. A large button labeled "Continue Securely" sat just below the message, inviting the user to verify their account details without hesitation.

Clicking the button led to a website nearly identical to the real company’s login page, but the URL was off by three characters. The form fields requested the user’s username, password, and even their social security number. Every detail on the page mirrored the official site, from the fonts and layout to the footer disclaimers, creating an almost perfect facade. The subtle misspelling in the URL was the only hint that something was amiss.

Behind the scenes, the credentials were captured before the redirect to the genuine site occurred. Shortly after, the stolen login information was used to access the account from a different IP address within the same session.

How Scam Messages Reach People -- and What They Actually Want

Scam messages work because they arrive inside something familiar. A carrier name. A bank logo. A recruiter tone. The FTC received more than 3 million fraud reports in 2025, and the common thread across nearly all of them is that the message looked routine right up until the moment it asked for something. A code. A payment. A login. A form that collected information the sender had no right to.

The FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center reported $20.9 billion in total cybercrime losses in 2025. The largest categories -- investment fraud, business email compromise, and phishing -- all rely on the same basic setup: a message that mimics something trusted, sent to enough people that a small percentage will act before they check. The message that reached you today is one of thousands sent from the same template.

The single most reliable protection is a pause before you act. Before you click a link, verify the destination. Before you reply with a code, confirm the request through the official website or app. Before you send money, call the number on the back of your card or listed on the company's real website. Scams are built around the window between when the message arrives and when someone stops to verify it. That window is where the losses happen.
